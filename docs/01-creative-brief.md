## 1. The Business

### 1.1 Identity
- **Name:** Soloex.io
- **Legal structure:** Women-owned, WBENC certified
- **Founded:** August 2024 (went full-time)
- **Co-founders:**
  - Derek Beitz — Technical lead, consulting, delivery
  - Kristin Beitz — Operations, books, billing
- **Headquarters:** Mechanicsburg, Pennsylvania
- **Geographic focus:** Primarily US, open to international

### 1.2 The name's private meaning
The name carries a three-layer meaning that is **not publicly disclosed on the site for now** but shapes the brand's spirit:

- **"Solo"** — From King Solomon. Wisdom. Confident counsel. Clients should feel at peace knowing they made a wise choice.
- **"Ex"** — From Exodus. Leaving bondage for the promised land. Derek left exploitative employment at a Summit Partner to build his own business. Clients are also in exodus — leaving broken CRM systems, outgrown tooling, bad implementations.
- **".io"** — Modern tech credibility. Signals software, AI era, and current industry.

**Brand archetype:** Sage / Guide. The wise counselor who has walked the path and leads others through.

**Faith treatment on the site:** Level A1 (quietly present). No public references to the biblical origin. The wisdom and guidance energy is baked into the voice and positioning, not explained. May be revisited later.

### 1.3 Positioning
Soloex is a Salesforce and HubSpot consultancy for companies in **CRM distress** — bad implementations, outgrown systems, and tech stack exhaustion. The firm migrates, rebuilds, and optimizes based on industry best practices and 100+ projects of hands-on experience.

**Target client profile:**
- **Company size:** Up to 500 employees (sweet spot: SMB and lower mid-market)
- **Role:** Decision-makers — founders, owners, VPs, directors with budget authority
- **Situation:** Already have a CRM that's broken, bloated, or not scaling
- **Industry:** Agnostic (experience spans universities, healthcare, tech, landscaping, automotive, and more)

### 1.4 Founding story (internal reference)
Derek worked as the technical closer at a Summit-tier Salesforce Partner. He joined sales calls to provide technical confidence, which won the business. He then ran the entire project — business analysis, solution architecture, project management, client communication, and upsell. The agency sold these projects for $170,000. Derek's salary was $93,000.

Derek started building his own client book after hours. When it grew large enough, he went full-time with Kristin in August 2024. This forms the core of the About page — a story of expertise and integrity, not a sob story. The animating phrase: *"No one was going to out-work me."*

## 2. Brand Voice

### 2.1 Voice dimensions (all locked)
| Dimension | Setting |
|---|---|
| Formality | Middle — professional but not stiff, accessible but not casual |
| Pronoun | "We" throughout (Soloex is a company, not a freelancer) |
| Confidence | Bold — no hedging, no "we think," no "we try" |
| Energy | Calm and grounded — wisdom, not hype |
| Archetype | Sage / Guide |

### 2.2 Representative voice sample
> *"Your CRM isn't broken because you chose the wrong tool. It's broken because someone built it wrong, abandoned it, or outgrew it. We've fixed 100+ of them. Yours won't be hard."*

### 2.3 What to avoid
- Bro-y hype language ("Let's crush your CRM!")
- Sales-y urgency ("Limited time!")
- Excessive disclaimers or hedging
- ALL CAPS anywhere
- Exclamation points as default punctuation
- Any faith references in public copy (for now)
- Claiming Soloex is Summit-tier (we are a **certified partner**, and Derek **trained at** a Summit Partner)
- "5 years of experience" — drop from stats strip entirely; use "100+ projects" instead

### 2.4 Accuracy guardrails
- ✅ **OK:** "Former consultant at a Summit-tier Salesforce Partner"
- ✅ **OK:** "Trained inside a Summit-tier agency before going independent"
- ✅ **OK:** "Certified Salesforce Partner"
- ❌ **Never:** "Summit-tier consultancy" / "Summit-tier work" / "Summit-tier expertise"

## 3. Visual Identity

### 3.1 Theme
- **Theme:** Light (not dark)
- **Primary background:** White (#FFFFFF)
- **Background texture:** Thin grid lines, subtle. SVG pattern — 40×40px cells, 0.5px stroke, navy at ~10% opacity. Echoes the gridded background in the original Soloex logo image.

### 3.2 Color palette
| Role | Color | Hex |
|---|---|---|
| Darkest navy (backgrounds, deep accents) | Darkest blue | `#051A2B` |
| Dark blue (primary text, major CTAs) | Dark blue | `#0C5494` |
| Medium blue (logo, accents, secondary CTA) | Medium blue | `#479DE7` |
| Light blue (accents on dark, soft text) | Light blue | `#A8D4FA` |

Body text on white will default to a slightly desaturated navy/charcoal such as `#051A2B` or a muted `#4A5A6B` for secondary copy. Primary CTAs are filled with `#0C5494` on white text.

### 3.3 Typography
- **Headlines:** Newsreader (Google Fonts) · weight 500
- **Body text:** Inter (Google Fonts) · weights 400 and 500
- **Numeric displays:** Inter (consistent with body)
- **Rationale:** Editorial serif headlines signal wisdom, authority, and permanence — the Sage archetype made visible. Newsreader specifically evokes the Stripe Press aesthetic Derek admires without the quirk of Fraunces or the neutrality of Source Serif. Inter is the world's most reliable body sans.

### 3.4 Logo
- Existing Soloex.io logo (diamond with flowing wavy lines, wordmark below)
- Two variants provided: with gridded dark navy background, and transparent/no background
- On the site, use the no-background variant over light surfaces
- Hidden "S" in negative space — do not over-explain

### 3.5 Reference sites Derek admires
- **getambassador.com** — logo carousel (grayscale → color on hover, right-to-left scroll), stats strip, three-pillar structure, direct copy
- **stripe.com** — clean, clear above-the-fold, story in one view, confident
- **clay.com** — similar clean aesthetic to Stripe
- **(Avoid):** Linear (too dark), Vercel (too blah), Basecamp (too cartoony), Ramp (broken scroll motion)

## 5. Engagement Process

The 6-step process used on the Process page:

| # | Step | What happens | Who leads |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Kickoff call** (free, 30 min) | Introductions. Low pressure. Mutual get-to-know. | Soloex |
| 2 | **Discovery call** | Focused working session. Client shares their screen and walks through current system. | Client leads tour; Soloex asks questions |
| 3 | **Scope and plan** | Soloex sends a technical scope document: recommendations, timeline, pricing. | Soloex |
| 4 | **Build** | Execution. Milestone-based (with weekly syncs) or retainer (hourly from monthly bucket). Client chooses model. | Soloex delivers; weekly client syncs |
| 5 | **Go live** | CRM launches. Team trained. Client owns the system. | Joint |
| 6 | **Ongoing support** | Available for questions, tweaks, new work. No aggressive close; relationship stays open. | Soloex on-demand |

**Why Kickoff and Discovery are separate:** Derek deliberately doesn't ask clients to share their screen on the first call. Screen-sharing without preparation puts people on defense and yields bad information. A relaxed kickoff first, then a prepared discovery session. This is a selling point — we surface it in the Process page copy.

| Item | Decision |
|---|---|
| Privacy Policy | Required. Covers contact form data, Cloudflare Web Analytics (cookieless), optional follow-up marketing emails with opt-out. To be generated at build time. |
| Terms of Service | Standard services terms. Governing law: Pennsylvania. To be generated at build time. |

| Accessibility target | Default high standards — WCAG 2.1 AA in practice. Keyboard navigation, color contrast, image alt text, semantic HTML. No formal audit. |

### 14.1 Marketing email compliance note
Because follow-up emails are planned for people who use the contact form:
- Every outbound email must include an unsubscribe option (even manual follow-ups — a line like *"Not interested? Reply STOP and we won't reach out again."*)
- Form submitters should not be added to a bulk newsletter list without a clearer opt-in
- When a proper newsletter is added, use a reputable tool (Mailchimp, Buttondown, ConvertKit) that handles unsubscribes automatically

## 17. Decisions That Are Easy to Change Later

Most visual decisions are one-line edits. Don't over-optimize these in planning:

- Background texture (grid lines ↔ dots ↔ watermark ↔ flat)
- Button colors and styles
- Section ordering on the homepage
- Exact headline copy
- Specific imagery
- Footer text

## 18. Decisions That Are Harder to Change

These cost real effort to reverse:

- Tech stack (switching away from Astro/Cloudflare later)
- Domain
- Fundamental brand positioning ("CRM distress" angle, Sage archetype)
- Primary brand name
